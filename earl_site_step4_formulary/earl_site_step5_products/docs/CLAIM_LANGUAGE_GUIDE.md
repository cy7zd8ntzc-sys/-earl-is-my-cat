# Product Claim Language Guide

## Cosmetic-friendly wording

Use language that describes cleansing, moisturizing, appearance, comfort, or feel:

- Supports healthy-looking skin.
- Cleanses without stripping.
- Helps skin feel hydrated.
- Helps skin look smoother.
- For redness-prone skin.
- Leaves skin feeling calm and comfortable.
- Helps reduce the look of dryness or roughness.

## Avoid for cosmetics

Do not use disease-treatment, structure/function, or antimicrobial language for cosmetic products:

- Treats acne, rosacea, eczema, seborrheic dermatitis, KP, fungus, or infection.
- Reduces inflammation.
- Repairs, restores, or rebuilds the skin barrier.
- Kills bacteria, fungus, yeast, mites, or viruses.
- Prevents breakouts.
- Regrows hair or eyelashes.
- Blocks UV or prevents sunburn.
- Removes wrinkles, increases collagen, regenerates cells, or lifts skin.

## OTC drug categories

If the product makes claims in these lanes, treat it as an OTC drug category unless counsel confirms otherwise:

- Sunscreen / SPF
- Acne medications
- Dandruff / seborrheic dermatitis shampoos
- Antiperspirants
- Antifungals
- Wart removers
- Hair regrowth products
- Skin protectants

## Affiliate disclosure language

Use near affiliate recommendations:

> Some third-party links may be affiliate links, meaning Earl Is My Cat may earn a commission if you purchase through them. Recommendations should be based on clinical judgment and product characteristics, not commission rates.

## Supplement language

If supplement products are eventually launched, structure/function claims need substantiation and the required disclaimer:

> These statements have not been evaluated by the Food and Drug Administration. This product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease.

Place this according to the current regulatory requirement and attorney review.
